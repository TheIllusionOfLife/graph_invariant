\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented Harmony, a framework for automated theory discovery in scientific
knowledge graphs.  The four-component Harmony metric---compressibility,
coherence, symmetry (redesigned as intra-type behavioural consistency), and
generativity---provides a principled, domain-agnostic quality signal for scoring
KG mutations, with an optional frequency component for hybrid analysis.  An LLM
proposer generates structured, falsifiable theory-level claims, which are
validated and archived in a MAP-Elites quality-diversity grid across an
island-model search topology.

Evaluation across seven KG domains (five hand-curated, two Wikidata-sourced)
with 10-seed cross-validation and four external KGE baselines reveals a nuanced
picture.  The frequency heuristic achieves the highest Hits@10 overall,
reflecting the strong inductive bias of edge-type distributions in small KGs.
Among embedding-based methods, Harmony-guided proposals show directional
improvements over DistMult-alone on Hits@10 in three of five domains
(not reaching statistical significance at $\alpha = 0.05$), with the
strongest signal on the denser Wikidata Materials KG.  A regime
characterisation analysis shows that Harmony's complementary value increases
with graph density and edge-type diversity, suggesting that the framework is
most beneficial for larger, structurally richer KGs.  Factor decomposition
shows strong contributions from Harmony scoring and MAP-Elites diversity, while
further controlled experiments are needed to isolate the LLM proposer's
independent contribution.  Backtesting against held-out edges serves as a
conservative diagnostic independent of the training objective.

Future work includes scaling to larger scientific KGs, extending the relation
type vocabulary, integrating literature-retrieval-based plausibility scoring,
domain-adaptive component weighting, and exploring multi-LLM ensembles across
islands for improved diversity.
